Who Will Hurt the Most by the Repeal of Obamacare in 2017?

By Nicole Lewis and Johanna Chisholm

December 9, 2016

On November 8th, 2016, America elected Donald J. Trump to be the next President of the United States. Among some of the more ominous promises that he campaigned for during these past 18 months, the one that stands to have the most impact on Americans from every walk of life is his promise to repeal and replace ObamaCare. Some 20 million Americans are now covered as a direct result of the 2010 law, which saw the creation of individual health insurance and, more importantly, the expansion of Medicaid for the poor. Medicaid coverage was expanded to all individuals and families with an income that was 138 percent below the federal poverty level. The old guidelines already provided coverage for the nation’s extremely poor. This meant that the updated expansion primarily saw America’s working poor, who had previously been one of the largest uninsured groups in the country, would now be eligible for Medicaid.

One of the suggestions that Trump’s administration has made in their plans to repeal and replace Obamacare would be to remove the expansion that allowed for poor Americans to gain coverage through Medicaid; this would immediately lead to 13 million people losing their health insurance coverage.

Hypothesizing that Trump does decide to take this unorthodox approach to repealing Obamacare, we have broken down which groups, broken down by race and ethnicity, would stand to lose the most.

As you can see from the percent change from 2010 to 2015, the Medicaid coverage for asian and hispanic groups increased by 41 percent. Assuming that most of these people were, as described in the Obamacare report, “poor, working Americans”, then you can expect that they will definitely be the most hurt from Trump removing the Medicaid expansion.
